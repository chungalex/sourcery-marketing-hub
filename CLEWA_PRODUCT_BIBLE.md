# PART ONE: THE PROBLEM

## What is actually happening

A brand founder in Los Angeles designs a denim jacket. They find a factory in Ho Chi Minh City through a friend of a friend, exchange a few messages, agree on a price, and wire $4,500 as a deposit. They have no signed contract. They have no documented specification. They have no formal milestone structure. They have no idea what happens if the goods arrive wrong.

They manage the entire production relationship — from that first message to the day the cartons arrive at their warehouse — on WhatsApp, a Google Sheet, and memory.

This is not unusual. This is how the overwhelming majority of physical product brands manage overseas production. Not because they are careless. Not because they do not care about quality or timing or money. Because the professional infrastructure for this specific relationship — the brand-to-overseas-factory relationship — does not exist at a price and complexity level that serves them.

What goes wrong, and it always goes wrong eventually: a spec changes in a WhatsApp message and never makes it into the production record. Final payment releases before goods are properly inspected because there is no gate enforcing the sequence. A cargo cutoff is missed because nobody was tracking the production countdown against the delivery window. A factory produces to the original spec, not the revised one, because the revision was a chat bubble that got lost in a thread of 4,000 messages. A season is missed. $40,000 is tied up in goods that are wrong. The brand has no leverage and no record.

The emotional reality of this moment — and it is a real, specific, devastating moment that thousands of brand founders experience every year — is what Clewa exists to prevent.

## Why nothing exists for this customer

The enterprise tools — SAP, Oracle, Infor, GT Nexus — cost $50,000 to $500,000 per year. They require dedicated IT implementation teams, sourcing departments, and operational infrastructure that a brand doing 500 units cannot justify. They are built for corporations, not founders.

The sourcing marketplaces — Alibaba, Faire, Made-In-China, Pietra — are phone books. They show you factories but have no infrastructure for working with one once you have found it. Discovery without operations.

The production management software — Katana, MRPeasy, Craftybase — is built for in-house manufacturers. Companies that own their production facility, manage their own shop floor, and schedule their own workers. None of them have a concept of an overseas factory relationship where a brand issues a PO and a factory somewhere in Vietnam manufactures to it. Wrong category entirely.

The sourcing agents — the humans who take 5-15% of production costs to manage the factory relationship — are the current solution for brands who can afford them. A brand doing 4 production runs per year at $20,000 average pays $10,000 to $30,000 per year for a human to do what Clewa does for $588.

The result: hundreds of thousands of physical product brands globally are managing their most important business relationship with tools that were never designed for it. They collectively represent billions in annual production value running through WhatsApp threads.

The gap is not a gap in features. It is a gap in category. Nobody has built the professional infrastructure for the brand-to-overseas-factory relationship at the right price point and complexity level.

Until Clewa.

# PART EIGHT: THE PRODUCT

## The complete lifecycle

Clewa covers every dimension of the factory relationship — from the moment a brand does not have a factory to the moment they have run twenty orders and built irreplaceable institutional knowledge.

**Find**
Registered manufacturer directory with OTIF scores from real completed orders. AI factory matching from a plain-language brief — "I need a Vietnam factory for a 300-unit selvedge denim jacket, 12oz, AQL 2.5, July delivery." Due diligence checklist at the point of factory selection: the questions experienced buyers always ask, what the answers should look like, what should concern you. Multi-factory RFQ — one brief sent simultaneously to multiple factories, quotes returned in a structured format, side-by-side comparison.

**Vet**
Factory verification system — certification requests, audit documentation, OTIF scoring from real transaction data. The "never self-reported" positioning is genuine: every OTIF score on Clewa is calculated from completed order records, not entered by the factory. Formal audit request mechanism. Factory review system from completed orders.

**Quote**
Multi-factory RFQ with AI quote normalisation. The AI reads every quote and flags when they are not apples-to-apples: "Factory A's price excludes washing, labels, and packing. Factory B's includes them. The real price difference is $1.20/unit, not $2.80." This alone saves experienced sourcing directors hours of spreadsheet work per season.

**Contract**
Auto-generated production contract at PO stage. Specific to the order — product, quantity, delivery window, QC standard, payment terms. Tied to platform-documented conditions. Not a template — a document generated from the actual agreed terms.

**Order**
Guided 4-step PO creation with contextual guidance at every decision point. Incoterms: plain-language explanation of FOB for a first Vietnam order. AQL: what 2.5 means in units for their specific order quantity. Milestones: why each gate exists and what it protects. Tet and holiday warnings: fires when delivery date falls near a factory shutdown window. Tech pack completeness check: flags missing fields before the PO is issued.

**Pay**
Milestone-gated payment releases. Deposit releases on PO issue. Sample gate: bulk cannot start until the brand formally approves the sample. Production milestone: optional intermediate release for long-run orders. QC gate: final payment blocked until QC passes the agreed AQL standard. Every release is manual — the brand confirms each one. Every release is logged permanently. Clewa does not hold funds — the brand pays the factory directly, but the gate enforces the sequence.

**Sample**
Factory submits sample photos to the order. Brand annotates directly on photos — circle the collar, add a comment, mark the specific issue. Formal approval is a one-click sign-off with timestamp, not "ok looks good" in a chat. Revision rounds are logged with factory acknowledgement — the factory must confirm they have received and understood each revision before the next round begins. Revision history is permanent.

**Produce**
SKU-level tracking. Factory milestone check-ins: cutting done, sewing 60% complete, finishing started. Production photos attached to milestones. Formal change order flow — every spec change is a documented request the factory must acknowledge before it becomes official. Timezone approvals across Vietnamese/Western working hours.

**QC**
AQL-based defect reporting. Defect type, severity, quantity, garment location, photo. AQL pass/fail calculated automatically from the agreed standard. If the defect rate exceeds the agreed AQL, final payment is blocked. Factory corrective action request formally logged. The quality standard the brand set at PO stage is the standard enforced at delivery.

**Dispute**
Formal dispute filing mechanism. Every dispute is attached to the order record with the full evidence trail — PO, tech pack, sample approvals, change orders, QC report, communications. The brand has a complete documented case before the dispute conversation even starts. Final payment blocked until dispute resolution is confirmed.

**Ship**
Carrier tracking links. Freight document checklist — adapts to incoterms and destination. Commercial invoice, packing list, bill of lading, certificate of origin — each checked before final payment. Estimated arrival notifications.

**Comply**
Supply chain compliance documentation exported in one click: CSDDD format, UFLPA chain-of-custody, Modern Slavery Act supplier declarations. Every order run on Clewa automatically builds the compliance record as a byproduct. No additional data entry required.

**Reorder**
Reorder intelligence from real lead time history. Safety stock calculator using actual completed order data — not industry averages. When Shopify is connected: exact reorder date calculated from real sell-through velocity combined with real factory lead time. "Issue FW26 PO by July 3 or you will stockout in October." Pre-populated order from the previous one. One click from alert to new PO.

# PART NINE: THE INTELLIGENCE LAYER

## The philosophy

AI is the nervous system inside every order — not a chatbot on top of the platform. It watches the data continuously. It speaks before being asked. It catches what humans miss. It tells the brand what to do before they know to ask.

The difference between a system of record and a system of intelligence is this: a system of record shows you what happened. A system of intelligence tells you what is about to happen and what to do about it.

## What it actually says

This is what the intelligence layer sounds like in practice:

*"HU LA Studios accepted your PO 6 days ago but has not sent a sampling timeline or confirmed fabric receipt. For denim jackets at this factory, sampling typically starts within 3 days of acceptance. This may indicate a capacity issue. Here is the message to send today."*

*"Your SS26 jacket has 19 days to delivery. QC has not been scheduled. Based on typical QC duration of 7 days and cargo cutoff requirements of 10 days before arrival, you have 2 days before the delivery window is at risk. Contact your factory today."*

*"Your PO specifies 12oz raw selvedge denim but the tech pack uploaded last week references 11oz fabric. These conflict. Before bulk cutting starts, confirm which specification is correct. An uncorrected conflict at this stage typically causes a revision round — 2-3 weeks and $500-2,000 in additional sample costs."*

*"Final payment release requested. Before releasing: QC report — uploaded and reviewed. Packing list — NOT uploaded. Carton photos — NOT uploaded. Final payment should not be released until both are confirmed."*

These are not generic alerts. They are specific to the order, the factory, the product, and the stage. That specificity is what makes the intelligence irreplaceable.

## The three levels

**Level 1 — Rule-based (built and working now)**
Five specific triggers watching real Supabase data. Fire when conditions are met. AI generates contextual language from the rule context.

1. Silent factory — PO issued, no factory response in 3 days
2. Delivery window compression — days remaining vs production stage mismatch
3. Payment gate checklist — documents missing before payment release
4. Spec conflict — PO specs vs tech pack notes conflict
5. Overdue stage — order in current status longer than typical

**Level 2 — Pattern-based (requires 5-10 orders per factory)**
Factory-specific historical patterns incorporated into alerts. "This factory typically responds in 4 hours. Three days of silence is unusual for them specifically. Follow up now."

**Level 3 — Predictive (requires 50+ completed orders)**
Seasonal patterns, factory capacity signals, quality trends, price movement indicators. The platform starts seeing things that no human holds in their head simultaneously.

Left panel: what the AI is watching. Live order signals — critical in red, warning in amber, on-track in green. Click any signal to open that conversation.

# PART FOURTEEN: THE REGULATORY LANDSCAPE

## CSDDD — EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive

Entered into force 2024. Requires companies operating in the EU to conduct due diligence on their supply chains — identifying and addressing human rights and environmental risks. Applies to large companies first, cascades to smaller companies through supply chain requirements.

What brands need: factory documentation, material sourcing records, supplier declarations, audit trails. What Clewa provides: all of this as a byproduct of normal order management. A brand using Clewa for production management is automatically building their CSDDD compliance record.

## UFLPA — Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act

Creates a rebuttable presumption that goods with any connection to the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region are produced with forced labour and cannot be imported to the US. Applies to cotton, polyester, and other materials that may originate in or pass through Xinjiang.

What brands need: documentation of their full material chain — factory, mill, spinning facility, cotton origin. What Clewa provides: the order record that forms the basis of a UFLPA response, factory documentation requests, material origin questionnaires.

## EU Digital Product Passports

The EU Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) will require product-level Digital Product Passports for textiles. Exact implementation dates are subject to delegated acts by product category — avoid claiming specific dates until confirmed.

What brands need: a structured record of their product's materials, manufacturing location, environmental impact, and end-of-life instructions. What Clewa provides: the production record that contains most of this information already — factory, country of manufacture, materials (BOM tracker), QC data. The export format needs to match the DPP specification.

**Positioning:** "Clewa helps brands prepare for incoming EU product passport and supply chain traceability requirements by capturing supplier, material, production, and compliance data at the source." This framing is accurate and does not overclaim specific timelines.
